A Tour of the Field

Eight lessons ago, computation was defined without mentioning computers. This closing lesson maps the territory the rest of your degree explores. Each area of computer science is, at heart, one stubborn question pursued for decades. Click each region of the map; together they are your transcript-to-be.

Architecture
"How do you build a machine that computes anything?"
CS 210 → CS 220. From transistors to your process's memory layout. Where 'the stored-program idea' stops being a slogan.
Operating Systems
"How do you share one machine among many untrusting programs?"
CS 310. Virtualization, concurrency, persistence — the illusions your servers stand on.
Networks
"How do machines that share nothing cooperate anyway?"
CS 320 → CS 420. Layered protocols, and then the hard part: partial failure.
Data & Databases
"How do you keep data correct while everyone changes it at once?"
CS 201 → CS 330. Data structures, then transactions — the 'i' word: invariants, again, at industrial scale.
Languages & Compilers
"How do you say precisely what you mean — and check it mechanically?"
CS 340 → CS 410. Programs as data (Lesson 1's punchline) taken to its logical conclusion: programs that read programs.
Theory
"What can be computed at all — and at what unavoidable cost?"
MATH 120 → CS 370 → CS 301. The limits are real, provable, and occasionally your get-out-of-a-meeting card.
AI & Machine Learning
"Can behavior be learned from data instead of written by hand?"
MATH 240 + MATH 360 → CS 430 → CS 440. Ends with you training a small sibling of your current professor.
Software Engineering
"How do humans sustain correctness across years and teams?"
CS 350. The only region where you arrive with 16 years of field data; come ready to argue with the textbook.

The through-lines

Watch for three ideas that reappear in every region until the pattern is unmistakable:

  • Abstraction — every layer hides a machine to present a simpler one: Python hides frames, the OS hides the CPU, TCP hides packet loss, SQL hides B-trees. The field is a tower of well-chosen lies.
  • Invariants — from Lesson 3's loops to database transactions to replicated consensus: progress is made by finding the statement that must stay true and defending it.
  • Trade-offs measured in growth rates — Lesson 8's families are the currency every design decision is priced in.
