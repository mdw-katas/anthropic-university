# MATH 120 — Discrete Mathematics

**Prerequisites:** MATH 110

## Course Description

The single most important math course in a CS degree. Logic, proof
techniques, sets, functions and relations, counting, graphs, and number
theory — the raw material of algorithms, databases, cryptography, and the
theory of computation. This course is where "programmer intuition" gets
formal names and rigorous tools.

## Learning Objectives

- Translate between English statements and propositional/predicate logic;
  evaluate validity.
- Construct proofs: direct, by contraposition, by contradiction, and by
  induction (including strong and structural induction).
- Work fluently with sets, relations (equivalence, partial orders), and
  functions (injective, surjective, bijective).
- Count: permutations, combinations, the pigeonhole principle,
  inclusion-exclusion.
- Model problems with graphs and trees and reason about their properties.
- Understand modular arithmetic well enough to follow hashing and
  cryptography.

## Topic Outline

1. Propositional logic: connectives, truth tables, logical equivalence.
2. Predicate logic: quantifiers, nested quantifiers, negating statements.
3. Proof techniques: direct, contraposition, contradiction, cases.
4. Mathematical induction; strong induction; structural induction (the proof
   technique behind recursion).
5. Sets, set operations, power sets, Cartesian products.
6. Functions and relations; equivalence relations and partial orders.
7. Counting: product/sum rules, permutations, combinations, pigeonhole,
   inclusion-exclusion.
8. Recurrence relations: modeling and solving them (the math behind
   analyzing recursive algorithms).
9. Graph theory: paths, cycles, connectivity, trees, bipartite graphs,
   graph coloring.
10. Number theory: divisibility, primes, modular arithmetic, GCD and the
    Euclidean algorithm.
